Why Write, Right?



While this may be easy to take for granted in our modern over-socialized era, the fact that humans learned how to intercommunicate with precision and efficiency was unprecedented in Nature. The creation of language was a pivotal leap in our evolution as a species. This phenomena took on a life of its own as we began to grok the value of communication and now we have a plethora of mediums that include oral storytelling, music, text, drawing/painting, plays, film, etc.



Of all the options for communication that language gave birth to, text is the most Lindy. Which put simply, means that it's likely to stick around for a long time because it already has stuck around for a long time. This becomes quite clear when you contrast how much our other mediums have changed throughout history. Traditional oral storytelling became downloadable podcasts. Hums and chants eventually led to Beethoven which then led to dubstep. Theatre went from Shakespeare to black & white films to 3D-IMAX movies that need nine-figure budgets. We started off with cave paintings and then got the Mona Lisa and now we have memes exchanged over iMessage. All of this happened while the Code of Hammurabi, The Holy Bible, and Darwin's On The Origin of Species remain relevant and accessible to this day.



Text is the only medium that has proven to be immune to any of these wild mutations over the course of history. Yes, the formats through which we read / write have evolved (from stone etchings to papyrus scrolls to the printing press to digital screens) and lexicons do change over time, but as a whole, the experience associated with reading and writing has remained undeniably constant. Writing may not be the densest nor the most entertaining method of sharing information but it is certainly the most robust. What text lacks in color and sound, it makes up for with rawness and purity.



If you want your message to pass the test of time and travel as far and wide as possible, your best bet would be to first write it down.
